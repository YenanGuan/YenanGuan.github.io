Article Sharing - Econometrics & Machine Learning

Article link - Why Machine Learning is more Practical than Econometrics in the Real World

I came across this article in the R-bloggers subcription. It is a great source to keep updated with any latest R packages and tutorials.

My undergraduate had several Econometrics courses and my first serious thought about what I want to do for my career came from it. The way that Econometrics unfolds the inferences and indications of important variables greatly interested me.

So when I first got to know about Machine Learning, I felt doubted, especially when the professor covered linear regression in only one lecture, while I spent quite a lot of time in undergraduate learning the very details of linear regression assumptions, BLUE (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator) deductions written on paper in both numerical and matrix forms, and all those endogeneity, heteroskedasticity issues.

It took me some time to change my way of thinking from Econometrics to Machine Learning in my master study (MS Customer Analytics at WashU). After some projects and the practicum, I gradually learned that while the goal is different for the analytics tasks, we sometimes choose not to scrutinize the very details of a model, but to focus on the accuracy if accuracy should be prioritized for our business goal, especially when it is time contrained in a professional setting and your audience mostly do not care about the mathematical details.
(But we definitely need to choose a model based on some primary requirements, like linear/non-linear, and those required feature engineering basics)

However still, my interests get aroused every time when I see any comparisons between Econometrics and Machine Learning.

The article took weekly Walmart sales by store and department as an example, fit 7 Econometrics time series models, and 4 Machine Learning Algorithms.

Econometrics Models with AutoTS()

DSHW — Double-Seasonal Holt-Winters
ARIMA — Autoregressive, integrated, moving average
ARFIMA — Fractionally differenced ARIMA
ETS — Exponential Smoothing State-Space Model
NN — Feed-forward neural network with a single hidden layer and lagged inputs
TBATS (Exponential smoothing state-space model with Box-Cox transformation, ARMA errors, Trend and Seasonal components)
TSLM — time series linear model with trend and seasonal components

Machine Learning Algorithms

AutoCatBoostCARMA() — CatBoost
AutoXGBoostCARMA() — XGBoost
AutoH2oGBMCARMA() — H2O Gradient Boosting Machine
AutoH2oDRFCARMA() — H2O Distributed Random Forest

Then the article summarized the performance:

Winner in Single Model Accuracy — TBATS (Econometrics model)
Runner-Up in Single Model Accuracy — Catboost (Machine Learning model)
Winner in Run time — ML is the big winner
Winner in Shorter-Horizon Accuracy — The Econometrics models dominate on shorter forecast
Winner in Longer Horizon Accuracy — The Machine Learning models dominate on longer forecast

The author held the view that “the run-time is a pretty significant factor for me”, and laid emphasis on what your business needs are, in terms of the computational time costs, and whether short term or a longer term forecasts are needed.
